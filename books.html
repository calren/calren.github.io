Modern Romance

When I read that Aziz had a book coming out, I decided I needed to treat myself.

To start, I think anybody contemplating buying the Kindle version should really just spend the extra bucks for the hardcopy, as the book is filled with cool colored pictures and graphs that I think really add to the overall experience. It just wouldn’t be as fun reading on a black and white Kindle.

That being said, when I first started the book I was expecting another biography similar to that of Mindy Khaling’s or Amy Poehler’s. Just another comedian writing a book about their lives in a humorous way while occasionally doing some name dropping of other famous people.

I’m glad that ended up not being the case at all. This book was actually written based on numerous research and study group experiments. Aziz teamed up with a renowned sociologist who is an expert in this field to learn as much as he can about modern dating. All of that made up for a very informing read backed by lots of data and interviews with real people. Of course, all this data is presented in an Aziz way - full of humor in the best way possible. Not once did I feel bored about all the numbers that were on the pages, and I found myself laughing out loud a lot every so often from the ridiculous stories Aziz came up with.

Most of the book is focused around online dating and how the current generation is navigating themselves through this new world. Why is it so hard to feel satisfied when there are so many options at our fingertips? How did people even find anybody before with their limited options? What happens after we finally settle on one person? Will we be more satisfied with our choices 20 years in the future?

This book tries to answer a lot of those questions by looking at older generations before us and how it eventually evolved to where we are now. Aziz even went to places like Japan, Paris and India to compare the dating cultures there. All of this combined created a very interesting perspective at how different dating can be.

There were a couple times in the book where I felt like things started to drag on from too much repetition. But never did I feel like the book was a drag to read, and I quickly finished this book within a couple hours of reading time.

All said and done, definitely take the moment to just treat yoself and read this book. If you don't know much about modern dating, you'll learn about it, and if you think you already know a lot about modern dating, you'll learn how to be better at it and then some.

Open

I had played tennis in high school, but the tennis stars that I've been familiar with were people like Federer, Nadal, and Djokivic. So I dived into this book not having any expectations. Despite all that, I was hooked from the first page of when Agassi described in detail the second to last match of his career. His thoughts, emotions, physical well-being, and everything else one can possibly describe.

At times I didn't have some trouble believe that he could possibly remember so many details from his life, and the uncanny coincidences that just seeme to happen. But I'll take them whole-heartedly because it makes the story so much more interesting as a whole.

Upon finish the book, I proceeded to spend a couple hours watching past Agassi matches on YouTube. Hours well spent.

Station Eleven

This book is on many "Best of 2014" lists, so I figured I should give it a read. Truth be told, I actually had a really hard time getting into the story in the beginning, and wasn't very engrossed until about 80 pages in. I think I was expecting more about how people would survive after the apocalyse, but the book focused more on the survivors' thoughts and memories.

The book goes back and forth between life before 99% of the population died, and life after. The center of the story is Authur, a kind of "washed out" actor who has a couple failed marriages, and no real career left. It was hard to care about Authur at first, which is perhaps why I didn't find much of the story interesting. But 1/3 through the book, the story turns to Authur's past, and that's when I finally started caring more about this character and sympathizing with him.

Much of the book is spent wondering how everybody is connected, and questioning why certain characters have certain secrets. The ending reveals all of this in an acceptable way, but nothing was too mind-blowing for me. It's like one of those romantic comedies where you realize in the end that everybody's story is strangely entertwined in some way.

In the end, I'm not convinced that this is one of the best books of 2014, but I guess it was better than average. I'm also pretty sure there are a bunch of religious comments/references that I completey missed, and perhaps that may have played a part in my under-appreciation of the book in general.

The Book Of Unknown Americans

I happened upon this book while was at Berkeley's Half Priced Books (one of my favorite bookstores of all time because going there always feels like going on a treasure hunt).

To my surprised, this book ended up being a lot more enjoyable that I had anticipated. One of the main reasons was probably because I tend to like the stories that are told through multiple characters. I always thought this type of story telling made stories more interesting because I get to experience the same event mulptiple times, each time with a new persperctive.

In this book, the story is told through immigrants young and old, each with their own unique backstories. Come to think of it, it reminds me a lot of the Netflix show, Orange is the New Black, with each chapter devoted to how a character came to be the way they are.

Perhaps another reason why I loved this book so much was because i was easily able to relate to it's stories. My parents are also immigrants from Taiwan, and reading about how the older generation had trouble assimiliating into the culture made me think about my own parents and the struggles they must have faced when they first came to America.

Despite having fragmented chapters that sometimes didn't contribute to the general plot of the story, the author was still able to craft a very satisfying and thought-provoking ending, which made me love this book even more.
